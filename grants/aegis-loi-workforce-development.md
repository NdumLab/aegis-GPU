Re: Letter of Intent for Aegis-GPU Pilot Support

Dear [Program Officer or Review Committee],

I am writing to express interest in funding support for Aegis-GPU, a browser-based AI infrastructure incident training and troubleshooting readiness platform designed for technical learners, early-career operators, and workforce-development programs preparing participants for GPU and distributed-systems support roles.

AI infrastructure environments are becoming more operationally complex, but training for GPU, CUDA, NCCL, scheduler, storage, and fabric troubleshooting remains fragmented across documentation, static labs, and ad hoc team knowledge. This creates a workforce readiness gap: learners may recognize commands without understanding which system layer owns a failure, when to preserve evidence, or how to avoid broad and unsafe remediation steps. As organizations expand AI and accelerated-compute environments, the need for safer, faster operator readiness is increasing.

Aegis-GPU addresses this problem by providing an interactive training environment that emphasizes evidence-first troubleshooting judgment rather than simple command memorization. The platform currently includes guided incident labs across hardware, software stack, networking, storage, and orchestration topics; reasoning scorecards that evaluate action safety and diagnosis quality; study-guide and quiz surfaces tied to AI infrastructure operations concepts; and branch-aware troubleshooting flows that distinguish clean, degraded, and unsafe operator choices. Representative browser-proof scenarios already validate key product paths including study guidance, Ask Aegis interaction, analytics transitions, and branching incident behavior.

We are seeking support for a pilot phase that would move Aegis from a validated prototype into a structured workforce-training implementation. Grant support would be used to strengthen pilot reporting, refine curriculum and assessment materials, harden product delivery, and evaluate measurable changes in troubleshooting readiness. The pilot would focus on learners preparing for AI infrastructure, platform operations, SRE, or technical support roles.

Our proposed pilot would serve an initial cohort of approximately 15 to 40 learners over a 6 to 8 week period. Participants would complete baseline assessment, guided incident-training modules, and endline evaluation. Success would be measured using outcomes such as correct layer identification, unsafe action reduction, clean incident completion rate, targeted quiz accuracy, and time to correct diagnosis. The goal is not only to expose learners to GPU operations concepts, but to improve their ability to make safe, evidence-based troubleshooting decisions in realistic AI infrastructure scenarios.

At this stage, Aegis should be presented as a credible, differentiated training platform with strong pilot potential, not as a market-ranked product with independently proven learning impact. That is precisely why grant support is timely: the project is far enough along to pilot seriously, but still at the stage where funding can directly improve deployment quality, evaluation rigor, and workforce impact evidence.

If invited to submit a full proposal, we would provide a detailed pilot plan, implementation timeline, budget narrative, and measurable outcomes framework aligned to your program priorities. We would welcome the opportunity to discuss fit with [Program Name] and whether Aegis is aligned with your goals in workforce readiness, technical training, or educational innovation.

Thank you for your consideration.
